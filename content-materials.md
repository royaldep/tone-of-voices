## Voice Guidelines

**Who is speaking:** Stacy. Personal twitter. Use 'I' not 'we'.

**Tone:** dry and sharp, not dunking. observational. practical. wry, not mean. specific. builder/operator. "I've seen this happen."

### Email tone
- For outreach emails, use a slightly more polished and softened tone than X posts
- Good phrasing in this lane: I’ve been looking into..., We’ve been working on..., At the moment, we’re offering..., If it sounds useful..., If that sounds helpful..., send it over for your thoughts, no pressure if it doesn’t feel like the right fit
- Keep it friendly and credible, smooth and specific, polished but not stiff
- Moving forward, email edits should not just smooth sentences. They should translate blunt utility language into softer, more polished, more considered language
- Preferred email transformations: concrete -> tangible, answer calls -> pick up calls, can't -> unavailable, book viewings -> schedule viewings, leads -> potential leads when helpful, busy -> tied up, main idea -> goal is simply, handle -> take care of, keep it running -> ongoing management, another thing to manage -> another task on your plate, do it -> approach it
- Use soft CTAs like: If you’re interested, just reply yes and I’ll send over a few details on how it would work / If that sounds helpful, just reply yes and I’ll send over a few details on how we’d approach it
- Let examples flow naturally in one sentence rather than choppy fragments
- In this email lane, goal/idea language can work when it sounds calm and practical: The goal is simply to...
- When editing for this tone, prefer the more premium, softened construction if it still sounds natural

**What it is NOT:**
- making fun of the business owner
- sarcastic for the sake of it
- gotcha energy
- not X but Y framing (AI-sounding)
- slow book pacing
- over-described
- stating the obvious

**What it IS:**
- naming friction clearly
- empathizing with how easy it is to miss
- pointing to the cost without blame
- show the scene, don't explain the insight

**Quick self-check before posting:**
- am I mocking the person or the problem?
- would this still sound ok if the owner read it?
- does this add a useful example or just attitude?
- if it adds signal, keep it. if it is mostly attitude, trim it.

**3 moves that keep edge without snark:**

1. easy to miss framing
"this happens a lot" / "super common" / "easy thing to miss" / "looks fine on desktop, breaks on mobile"
sounds experienced, not judgy.

2. quiet cost framing
"so close and then gone" / "looks small but gets expensive fast" / "everything works except the part that gets the booking"
strong and not mean.

3. systems over blame framing
"the issue is the flow" / "they are doing the hard part (getting attention), then the page drops it" / "not a traffic problem first, a clarity problem first"
sounds smart and fair.

**Formatting rules:**
- No em dashes, no hyphens, no double quotes
- Parentheses ok
- No banned words: real, really, actually, feels, feel, quiet
- Fast pacing. a thought, not a sentence.
- Vulnerable only if true. do not claim experiences that have not happened yet.

**Where we are:** early stage, looking for first customers, doing manual outreach, learning as we go. that is the story.

## Target Audience Insights

### Restaurants
- Pain: after-hours calls and DMs go unanswered = lost reservations
- Language: "guests," "reservations," "private dining," "walk-ins," "front of house"
- Scenario: phone rings at 10pm, no one answers, lead lost by morning

- Pain: leads come in during jobs — can't answer, competitor gets it
- Language: "quote requests," "on a job," "Sunday inquiries," "follow-up"
- Scenario: homeowner sends DM Sunday, you're busy, they call someone else Monday

### Indie Hackers / SaaS founders
- Pain: building the product AND doing customer support AND social = burnout
- Language: "solo," "shipping," "support queue," "DMs," "inbound"
- Scenario: you shipped a product but spend 2hrs/day on repetitive messages

### Real Estate
- Pain: listing leads go cold fast if not responded to quickly
- Language: "listings," "showing," "leads," "inquiries," "follow-up"
- Scenario: someone DMs about a listing while you're showing another property

## Niche Analysis Framework (Mar 17 2026)

Use this to evaluate niches for software replacement / automation opportunities.

### Template
1. **Niche**
   - What is the niche?
   - Who are the businesses?
   - How big / small are they usually?

2. **Current software / current behavior**
   - What tools are they using now?
   - What are they paying for?
   - What are they still doing manually?

3. **Sharpest pain**
   - What specific part hurts the most?
   - Where do leads, time, or money get lost?
   - What repeats often enough to matter?

4. **Replaceable wedge**
   - What small part can we replace first?
   - What is narrow enough to be believable?
   - What does not require replacing their whole stack?

5. **Starter offer**
   - What is the easiest thing to say yes to?
   - Free tier?
   - Pilot?
   - Limited feature?
   - Limited volume?
   - One channel first?

6. **Expansion path**
   - Once the wedge works, what comes next?
   - More channels?
   - More automation?
   - More locations?
   - More workflow coverage?

7. **Money story**
   - How does this save money?
   - How does this make money?
   - What software spend can it replace?
   - What staff time can it reduce?
   - What missed leads can it recover?

8. **Pitch angle**
   - What is the simplest version of the pitch?
   - What exact pain / tool / workflow are we naming?

9. **Creative reach method**
   - How can we reach them without sounding like generic sales?
   - Audit?
   - Free first setup?
   - Workflow review?
   - Niche-specific example?
   - Cost-savings angle?

10. **Discovery questions**
   - What do we need to learn before pitching harder?
   - What tool do they use now?
   - What part do they hate?
   - What do they rely on most?
   - What would make them switch?

11. **Risks**
   - What makes this niche hard?
   - Switching cost?
   - Trust barrier?
   - Compliance?
   - Low urgency?
   - Hard integrations?

12. **Verdict**
   - Is this worth testing?
   - Why?
   - What should the first experiment be?

### Short version
niche -> current tools -> sharpest pain -> replaceable wedge -> starter offer -> expansion path -> money story -> pitch -> reach method -> risks

### Example: Google / Yelp auto-responder wedge
- **Niche:** local service businesses
- **Current behavior:** Google Business messages, Yelp, manual replies, staff answering repetitive questions
- **Sharpest pain:** high-intent inquiries sit too long or get handled in fragments
- **Replaceable wedge:** Google + Yelp auto-responder
- **Starter offer:** free first 50 inquiries
- **Expansion path:** Instagram, Facebook, X, website chat
- **Money story:** fewer missed leads, less staff time, lower software spend than Podium-style stack
- **Pitch angle:** start with Google and Yelp for free, expand if useful
- **Creative reach method:** message them after seeing slow responses, bad reviews, or unanswered listing questions
- **Risks:** if inquiry volume is too low, value may not feel urgent
- **Verdict:** strong wedge, easy to test

## Restaurant Review Pattern Insights (Mar 10 2026)

### Core takeaway
Restaurants are not mostly losing people because every problem is huge. They are losing people because too many small problems go unanswered, unexplained, or unfinished.

### Patterns that kept showing up
1. **The food is often not the whole problem in the review**
   A lot of places still had guests saying the food, drinks, or concept were good. What pushed the review negative was what happened around the experience: confusion, silence, delay, dismissal, or no follow up after something went wrong.

2. **Small operational misses become big emotional reactions**
   Wrong item, long wait with no update, missing side, reservation confusion, surprise fee, no answer after a complaint. On paper these sound small. In reviews, they become never again, ruined the night, or we will not be back.

3. **High expectations make service recovery matter more**
   For places with hype, high prices, strong branding, or a special occasion vibe, guests are less forgiving. If expectations are high, even a basic breakdown in communication lands much harder.

4. **The gap is often lack of closure**
   Something went wrong, the team was busy, nobody clearly explained what happened, and nobody followed up cleanly after. That unfinished ending seems to drive a lot of public reviews.

5. **Busy moments are where the damage happens**
   Rush periods, peak nights, brunch, delivery windows, reservation crunch, counter service rush. The issue is often not that the restaurant never cares. It is that the system breaks when volume hits.

6. **Guests react badly to uncertainty**
   People can tolerate a wait, a fee, a missing item, or a policy they do not love if it is explained clearly. What they react badly to is confusion: not knowing what is happening, what is included, who to ask, or whether anyone is going to help.

7. **A lot of bad reviews are expectation mismatch reviews**
   This showed up with pricing, add ons, reservation timing, bar seating, takeout, service charges, dessert timing, and what guests thought they were getting. Clearer expectations up front would likely prevent a lot of frustration.

8. **Many restaurants attempt recovery, but too late**
   Sometimes the owner apologizes, gives a discount, or replies publicly. But by then the guest has already posted, told the story, and decided how they feel. The missed window is right after the issue happens.

9. **Best outreach angle**
   Not AI for restaurants.
   The stronger angle is practical: when something goes wrong, guests do not have a clear way to get help fast or get a clean follow up.

10. **Best fit restaurants are not just places with bad reviews**
   Look for restaurants where the review pattern shows guest facing friction, a problem the offer can realistically help, enough signal to personalize, and ideally a repeated pattern instead of one random complaint.

### Useful framing lines
- The operational issue happens first. The emotional reaction comes from what happens after.
- What people react badly to is uncertainty.
- That is often the part people remember most.
- On its own, each missed moment looks minor. Together, it is a lot.
