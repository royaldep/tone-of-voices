## Writing & Tone Rules

- No em dashes, no hyphens in copy, no double quotes
- No snark or sarcasm. wry is ok, dunking is not.
- No "not X but Y" framing (AI-sounding)
- No shaming the business owner. name the problem, not the person.
- Casual, human tone. fast pacing. a thought, not a sentence.
- Banned words: real, really, actually, feels, feel, quiet
- Stacy is non-native English speaker, keep it simple and direct
- Tone sweet spot: dry and sharp, observational, practical, "I've seen this happen"
- Quick check: mocking the person or the problem? would the owner feel ok reading it?
- Show the scene. do not explain the insight.

## Email Tone

- For outreach emails, Stacy likes a slightly more polished and softened tone than X posts
- Good email phrasing: I’ve been looking into..., We’ve been working on..., At the moment, we’re offering..., If it sounds useful..., If that sounds helpful..., send it over for your thoughts, no pressure if it doesn’t feel like the right fit
- Email tone should be friendly and credible, smooth and specific, polished but not stiff
- Moving forward, email edits should not just smooth sentences. They should translate blunt utility language into softer, more polished, more considered language
- Preferred email transformations: concrete -> tangible, answer calls -> pick up calls, can't -> unavailable, book viewings -> schedule viewings, leads -> potential leads when helpful, busy -> tied up, main idea -> goal is simply, handle -> take care of, keep it running -> ongoing management, another thing to manage -> another task on your plate, do it -> approach it
- Use soft CTAs like: If you’re interested, just reply yes and I’ll send over a few details on how it would work / If that sounds helpful, just reply yes and I’ll send over a few details on how we’d approach it
- Keep examples flowing naturally in one sentence rather than choppy fragments
- In this email lane, goal/idea language can work when it sounds calm and practical: The goal is simply to...
- When editing for this tone, prefer the more premium, softened construction if it still sounds natural

## Tweet Writing Notes (Fountain of Scale)

### What works
- Specific, grounded language (real restaurant scenarios)
- Leads with the benefit, not the problem
- Short, confident sentences
- Feels like a founder talking, not a marketer

### What to avoid
- Generic AI-sounding phrases ("disappears into the background", "works best when")
- Analogies that could make hospitality workers feel replaced or undermined (ex: sous chef comparison)
- "Not X but Y" framing (shaming by contrast)
- Snark
- Em dashes

### Approved tweets (these are EXAMPLES of the right tone/style, NOT templates to reuse or remix)
- "if a restaurant owner has to think about us, we haven't done our job yet."
- "the goal was never to be noticed. it was to make sure nothing gets missed."
- "we want restaurants focused on their guests. everything else, we'll handle."
- "restaurants that respond fast build trust fast. speed of reply has become part of the guest experience whether we think about it that way or not."
- "the future of restaurants is operators spending more time on the floor and less time in their inbox. we're here for that shift."
- "AI in hospitality works best when it handles the repetitive stuff so your team can focus on what they're actually great at. presence, warmth, the guest in front of them."

**Expertise (examples of right style, not templates)**
- "automating after-hours inquiries is one of the highest ROI things a restaurant can do. the leads are already coming in. just make sure someone's there to catch them."
- "speed of reply is part of the guest experience now. a fast response to a DM builds trust before they even walk in."
- "your Instagram comments are part of your front of house. worth treating them that way."
- "a phone call at 10pm used to mean a lost reservation by morning. not anymore."

**Building in Public (examples of right style, not templates)**
- "we're building Fountain of Scale because we kept seeing great restaurants leave money on the table after hours. fixing that felt worth doing."
- "two months in and the thing we keep hearing from operators: 'i just want it to work without me touching it.' building exactly that."
- "early lesson from building this: restaurant owners don't want to learn new software. they want the problem solved. keeping that close."

**Social Proof (examples of right style, not templates)**
- "one restaurant handled 3 private dining inquiries in a single night automatically. leads captured, follow-ups flagged, nothing slipped through."
- "5 Instagram comments, 3 DMs, 3 phone calls. one Monday. zero staff hours spent on any of it."
- "when your team walks in Tuesday morning and the weekend inquiries are already sorted, that's the feeling we're building toward."

### QRT / Quote Tweet style
- Lead with the insight, not the company
- "the gap between when guests reach out and when restaurants respond is where trust gets lost." ✅
- "this is exactly why we built Fountain of Scale." ❌ too salesy
- "this stat doesn't surprise us. been seeing it firsthand." ❌ too insider/smug

### Why we rejected specific tweets
- "best restaurant tech disappears into the background" -> too generic, AI-sounding
- sous chef analogy -> could make kitchen staff feel compared to/replaced by software
